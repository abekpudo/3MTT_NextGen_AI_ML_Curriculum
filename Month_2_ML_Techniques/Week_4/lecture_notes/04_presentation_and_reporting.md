 Presentation & Reporting: Telling the Data Story

 1. Overview
The final step of any machine learning project is communicating your findings. Your audience might range from fellow data scientists to business executives who have never seen a line of code. You must be able to translate "F1-Score" into "Business Value."

 2. The "Problem-Solution-Impact" Framework
A great capstone presentation follows this narrative arc:
1. The Hook (Problem): Why should we care? (e.g., "Company X is losing ₦10M a month due to customer churn").
2. The Journey (Approach): How did you solve it? (Data cleaning, feature engineering, model selection). Keep this high-level for non-technical audiences.
3. The Result (Impact): What did you find? (e.g., "Our model can identify 85% of churners before they leave, potentially saving ₦8.5M").

 3. Tailoring Your Message

 3.1. For Technical Peers
- Focus on: Hyperparameter tuning, feature importance, cross-validation strategy, and model limitations.
- Visuals: ROC curves, Precision-Recall curves, Residual plots.

 3.2. For Non-Technical Stakeholders
- Focus on: The "bottom line," reliability, and the action they should take based on the model.
- Visuals: Simple bar charts, "Before vs. After" comparisons, and the live demo.

 4. Visual Storytelling Tips
- Less is More: Don't put 500 words on a slide. Use one powerful chart per slide.
- Annotate: Don't just show a graph; put a circle around the most important part and add a caption (e.g., "Notice the spike in errors for users over 60").
- Consistency: Use the same colors and fonts throughout your deck and your Streamlit app.

 5. The "Post-Mortem" (Limitations & Next Steps)
A professional report always includes:
- What didn't work? (e.g., "Linear models failed to capture the non-linear relationship in the price data").
- What's next? (e.g., "Collecting more data on user location would likely improve accuracy by 10%").
